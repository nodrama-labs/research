* Description

Literature review covering the four papers in =references/=, motivated
by the negative result in [[file:regime_student_t_2022.org][regime_student_t_2022.org]]. Goal: map each
paper's methodological choices (features, state count, transitions,
covariates, frequency) to the specific failure modes our Phase-0 run
exhibited, and use that mapping to design a follow-up that keeps the
same data and the same comparison frame but changes what plausibly
fixes the failure.

We are not synthesising the entire HMM-in-crypto literature — only what
these four papers tell us about choices our notebook made.

* Recap: what Phase-0 actually showed

The Phase-0 hypothesis was that a 3-state Student's-t HMM, fit to BTC
*daily log-returns* over 2021-06 → 2023-06 with μ-sort labelling, would
identify 2022 as a sustained bear. It did not. Concretely:

- Student's-t Viterbi placed 100 % of 2022 (and 100 % of 2021-H2 bull
  and 100 % of 2023-H1 recovery) into a single "ranging" state.
- ν̂ collapsed to ≈4 in every state under bounds [3, 30] — uniformly
  fat-tailed, not regime-dependent fat-tailed.
- Gaussian and Student's-t both bound "bear" by μ-sort to the small
  cluster of crash *days* (LUNA, Celsius, FTX), not bear *months*.
- The model is correctly reporting that daily 2022 returns look like a
  fat-tailed walk near zero — the same as every other post-2021 window.
  The feature is the problem, not the model.

The four candidate fixes implicit in that diagnosis were: change the
*feature*, change the *state count*, allow the *transitions to vary*,
or change the *frequency*. The literature touches all four.

* Paper 1 — Shih, Huang, Hsu (2024). Bitcoin Cycle through Markov Regime-Switching Model

** Setup
- Asset: BTC (USD, weekly).
- Window: Jan 2018 → Sep 2022 (~250 weekly obs).
- Model: classic Hamilton MS-AR(q) with 2 states (high-growth, low-growth),
  Gaussian shocks, fixed transition matrix.
- Four variants: BTC-only (Model I), + VIX (II), + USD index (III),
  + both (IV) — covariates enter the *mean equation*, not the
  transition matrix.

** Fitted parameters (Model I, BTC-only)
- σ₁ = 0.007 (high-growth), σ₂ = 0.053 (low-growth) — ratio ≈ 7.5×.
- μ₁ = 0.010, μ₂ = 0.091 (note: μ₂ > μ₁; "bear" here means high-σ +
  high mean, i.e. *turbulent positive-drift* rather than sustained
  negative drift — same gotcha our μ-sort labelling tripped on).
- p₁₁ = 0.975, p₂₂ = 0.881 → expected durations 40 vs 8 weeks.
- Adding USD index (Model III) extends bull duration 40 → 48 weeks,
  bear 8 → 10 weeks. Both VIX and USD enter with negative coefficients
  on the BTC mean.

** What it implies for our setup
- *Frequency*: weekly aggregates 5 daily observations into one. That
  averages out the crash-day spikes that captured our "bear" label.
  Their LG/HG separation works partly because of this smoothing.
- *State count*: K = 2 is enough at weekly frequency; we tried K = 3
  at daily and the third state degenerated.
- *Labelling*: their HG/LG is σ-driven, not μ-driven. In a heavy-tailed
  regime the right contrast is volatility, not mean. Sort states by σ
  (or by σ × −μ) rather than μ.
- *Covariates in mean*: weakest form of "non-homogeneous" — does not
  let the transition matrix shift with VIX/USD, just lets the
  conditional mean re-centre. Worth checking but unlikely to fix our
  problem because in 2022 BTC daily returns the cross-correlation
  with VIX/USD is modest at the noisy daily scale.

** Verdict for follow-up
Switching to weekly is a real-but-blunt fix: it shrinks the sample
from 759 daily obs to ~108 weekly, which makes K = 3 marginal and K = 4
infeasible. Useful as a robustness check; not the primary path.

* Paper 2 — Pakštaitė et al. (2025). Bayesian MCMC + (NH-)HMM with macro covariates

** Setup
- Asset: BTC (USD, daily). Window: Jun 2016 → Dec 2024 (~3100 obs).
- Observation: *log-transformed scaled price* — explicitly not log-returns.
- Models: 2-state HMM (homogeneous) and NH-HMM (transitions logistic-
  parameterised in covariates via Pólya–Gamma augmentation).
- 16 candidate covariates (macro: SPX, DJI, IXIC, VIX, INFL,
  EURUSD/GBPUSD/JPYUSD/CNYUSD, GC_F, CL_F; BTC-specific: VOL, BLOCK,
  HASH, MINER, HALVING), lagged 7 days, selected by Bayesian MCMC with
  a double reversible-jump algorithm and a complexity penalty.
- Rolling 100-day window for fitting; 1/5/30-step bootstrapped forecasts.

** Key empirical findings
- Optimal covariate set is *time-varying*: in 2016–2019 BTC-specific
  variables dominate (HALVING, INFL, GC_F); in 2019–2024 macro
  variables dominate (CNYUSD, GBPUSD, EURUSD, DJI, HASH). Post-2019
  BTC behaves more like a macro-sensitive asset.
- A 100-day window matches "a single hidden state typically persists
  for 30–60 days" — chosen explicitly so each window sees both states.
- NH-HMM (with VIX in the transition equation) does *not* uniformly
  beat the homogeneous HMM at 1-step-ahead; macro covariates in the
  emission equation help more than in transitions at short horizons.
- Best 1-step MAPE in the recent (2019–2024) sample: 25.85 % for the
  homogeneous-HMM Set 1 (CNYUSD, DJI, GC_F, VOL).
- LASSO, Elastic Net, Random Forest, and rolling SVM all underperform
  the HMM framework for this task — the multicollinearity in macro
  factors plus the regime-switching structure are not handled well by
  generic shrinkage.

** What it implies for our setup
- *Feature choice*: modelling *log-price* (level) instead of *log-return*
  is the simplest change that gives the latent state something
  cumulative to anchor to — our negative-result diagnosis explicitly
  flagged "regime structure lives at the cumulative-price level, not
  the per-day-return level". Paper 2 validates this as a working
  choice with the same K = 2 / Gaussian emission family.
- *Window length*: 759 daily obs (our 2-year span) is shorter than
  their 100-day rolling but it's only enough for ~12–25 regime
  occurrences at their 30–60-day persistence. Tight but workable for
  a 2 or 3-state model; not for 4.
- *Covariates*: the best add at our window is probably DJI/CNYUSD/
  GC_F (per their recent-sample MCMC selection) plus VIX if going
  non-homogeneous.

** Verdict for follow-up
The cheapest, most-targeted change to our existing notebook is:
re-fit the same Gaussian and Student's-t machinery with *log-price* as
the observation, keeping K = 2 (their setup) and K = 3 (our setup) as
parallel runs. Same forward-backward, same Baum-Welch driver — only
the input array changes.

* Paper 3 — Koki, Leonardos, Piliouras (2020). 4-state NH-HMM with macro predictors

** Setup
- Assets: BTC, ETH, XRP. Daily *percentage* log-returns
  (100 × Δlog-price). BTC window: Jan 2014 → Nov 2019 (~2100 obs).
- 12 candidate predictors: EUR/USD, GBP/USD, CNY/USD, JPY/USD, Gold,
  Oil, SP500, VIX, 10Y Treasury Yield (TY), BTC size, BTC Hash, AR(1).
- Compares 12 models per coin: NHHMM with m ∈ {2,3,4,5}, HHM with m ∈
  {2,3,4,5}, plus 2-state MS-RW, random walk, AR(5), Kitchen Sink.
- Bayesian MCMC inference with Pólya–Gamma augmentation on the
  multinomial-logit transition probabilities; reversible-jump on
  transition predictors.
- Out-of-sample evaluation: CRPS and MSE at horizons 1, 2, 7, 15, 30.

** Headline empirical findings
- *Best model for BTC is 4-state NH-HMM* (lowest average CRPS across
  horizons, 1.78 vs 1.85–1.98 for benchmarks).
- BTC state structure (Table 6, in % log-returns; std also in %):

  | State | Occupancy | Mean (%) | Std (%) | Interpretation                |
  |     1 |     0.35  |   −0.46  |   5.91  | bear/turbulent (negative μ, high σ) |
  |     2 |     0.30  |   +0.49  |   2.49  | bull, high-kurt                |
  |     3 |     0.24  |   +0.47  |   1.69  | bull, low-kurt                 |
  |     4 |     0.11  |   +0.02  |   0.53  | calm / auxiliary               |

  States 2 and 3 share an almost-identical mean and differ only in
  *kurtosis* — i.e. the model uses 4 states to encode (bear, bull-vol,
  bull-quiet, calm). With K = 3 you cannot distinguish bull-vol from
  bull-quiet, so EM either (a) collapses both into one Gaussian-ish
  bull and over-flips, or (b) lets the Student-t emission absorb both
  via a uniformly fat tail — which is exactly what our notebook saw.
- Transition predictors with posterior inclusion probability > 0.4:
  VIX (1.00) and TY (0.90). VIX is the universal one across BTC/ETH/XRP.
- Predictors in the emission (regression on returns conditional on
  state) are mostly insignificant — non-linear effects come through
  the *transition* probabilities, not the mean.
- They explicitly note that m > 5 is worse (parsimony loss), and that
  removing insignificant emission predictors degrades out-of-sample
  performance even though they are not individually significant
  (consistent with the regime-switching ensemble argument).

** What it implies for our setup
- *State count*: K = 4 is the literature-validated choice for BTC
  daily returns. Our K = 3 was the smallest plausible setting; it is
  also one short.
- *Labelling*: state 1 *is* low-μ + high-σ (concordant with our μ-sort
  rule) but the interesting decomposition is between states 2 and 3,
  which share a mean. A μ-only sort cannot separate them; need a
  μ-then-σ tie-breaker or a kurtosis-aware label rule.
- *Heavy tails*: state 1 σ = 5.91 % vs state 4 σ = 0.53 % — that's an
  11× ratio. With Gaussian emissions this is feasible at K = 4
  because the extreme component absorbs tail observations without
  poisoning the others. With K = 3 and Student-t we observed the
  reverse: the fat-tail flexibility absorbed crash days into the
  modal component because there was no fourth bucket to send them to.
- *NH transitions*: VIX is the dominant transition predictor across
  all three coins. If we go non-homogeneous, VIX is the single
  predictor that buys the most.

** Verdict for follow-up
The single highest-leverage change is K = 3 → K = 4 (with or without
heavy tails), keeping emissions univariate-Gaussian. Paper 3 makes a
strong case that the bull decomposes into two sub-states our K = 3 run
cannot represent, and that this is the structural reason behind the
"one big ranging state" failure.

* Paper 4 — Malekinezhad & Rafati (2026). NHHMM on 4-hour BTC, multivariate observation

** Setup
- Asset: BTC/USDT, *4-hour candles*. Window: Jan 2024 → Jan 2026
  (~4380 obs).
- *Multivariate observation vector*: log return + rolling realised
  volatility + normalised trading volume.
- Models compared: 2-state HMM, 3-state HMM, 3-state NHHMM with
  time-varying transitions. EM for HMM; modified EM (kernel/local
  likelihood) for NHHMM. Viterbi for state inference.
- States named ex-ante: low-vol consolidation, high-vol bullish
  expansion, high-vol bearish correction.

** Results

  | Model    | LL    | AIC   | BIC   | Persistence | Vol RMSE |
  | HMM K=2  | −6125 | 12270 | 12355 | medium      |   0.021  |
  | HMM K=3  | −5874 | 11780 | 11910 | high        |   0.018  |
  | NHHMM K=3| −5712 | 11470 | 11620 | very high   |   0.015  |

- Mean regime durations: consolidation 6.2 days, bullish 4.7 days,
  bearish 3.9 days.
- 3-state beats 2-state across all metrics; NHHMM beats HMM at the
  same K. Consistent with paper 3's NH-HMM dominance result on daily
  returns, and with paper 2's finding that time-varying transitions
  matter.

** What it implies for our setup
- *Multivariate observation* is the clean experimental knob the
  literature uses to give the latent state more than one signal to
  bind to. Daily log-return alone is the *minimum*-information
  observation; (return, realised σ, log-volume) is the standard
  multivariate one.
- *Higher frequency* (4-hour) does *not* materially change the
  3-state structure — it just gives more observations per regime. At
  daily we have 759 obs; at 4-hour we'd have ~3036 over the same
  window (4× more), which would let K = 4 be viable.
- *Duration dependence*: paper 4 explicitly raises hidden semi-Markov
  models (negative-binomial / Poisson sojourn distributions) as the
  remedy when geometric sojourn is too memoryless. This is mentioned
  as Phase-2 in our original plan; the literature endorses it but
  also shows you can mostly fix the geometric-sojourn issue with
  non-homogeneous transitions (NHHMM) first.
- *Channel frequency ≠ HMM frequency*: paper 4 picks 4-hour data
  because regime durations of ~4–6 days require sub-week sampling
  resolution to be visible. Our 2022-bear target regime is ~250
  trading days long, so the latent chain does not need sub-daily
  samples. However, the *quality* of the σ channel in a (return, σ)
  bivariate observation can be upgraded without changing the HMM
  frequency: replace σ_t = std(r_{t-4:t}) (5 daily returns, noisy
  estimator) with realised variance σ_t = sqrt(Σᵢ r²_{t,i}) computed
  from N intra-day returns within day t (Andersen-Bollerslev-Diebold-
  Labys, 2001). Standard error on σ_t collapses by ~√N — N = 78 for
  5-min bars, N ≈ 6 for hourly. The result is still a once-per-day
  number that slots into the daily HMM unchanged; only one column of
  the observation matrix changes. Cost: ~50-line extension of
  ~data/fetch_btcusdt_daily.py~ to also pull hourly (or 5-min) bars
  over the window. Binance klines are free and go back to 2017.

** Verdict for follow-up
Multivariate observations + NHHMM (with VIX from paper 3 in the
transitions) is the most-aligned-with-literature setup. The smallest
plausible variant we could fit with current code is:
*bivariate observation (return, 5-day realised σ) + Gaussian emissions
+ homogeneous K ∈ {3, 4}*. Going to NHHMM requires logistic-transition
M-step + a VIX series.

* Proposed follow-up — ranked by code reuse vs likelihood of pass

The original Phase-0 setup (window 2021-06 → 2023-06; pass criteria:
2022 bear-posterior ≥ 0.6, flips ≤ 6, gap-or-flip-reduction) is
retained. Same window, same data, same EM driver, same restart
strategy. What changes is one knob at a time.

** Tier 1 — single-knob changes, < 100 lines of new code

1. *Log-price observation, K ∈ {2, 3}, Gaussian + Student-t* (paper 2).
   Change is one line in the data-prep cell: replace
   ~df["log_return"]~ with ~np.log(df["close"]) − np.log(df["close"][0])~
   (or a rolling z-score, paper 2's "scaled log-price"). Everything
   else — forward-backward, Baum-Welch, ν-update, restart jitter,
   Viterbi — reuses as-is. Risk: log-price is non-stationary and EM
   may detect "year 1 vs year 2" as the latent state rather than
   bull/bear. Mitigation: detrended log-price (subtract a 200-day MA)
   or paper 2's scaling.

2. *K = 4, daily returns, Gaussian + Student-t* (paper 3).
   Change is one line: ~K = 3~ → ~K = 4~. Sort+labelling rule needs
   a fourth slot ("bear", "ranging-vol", "ranging-quiet", "bull" or
   similar) — small refactor of the regime-name tuple. Risk: K = 4
   on 759 obs is sample-size-tight, especially with EM restarts.
   Mitigation: drop ν floor to 2.5 (paper 3 uses uniform priors) and
   accept the ν → ∞ limit on the "calm" state.

** Tier 2 — moderate refactor, ~200-300 lines

3. *Bivariate (return, realised σ_5) emission* (paper 4 — the
   multivariate idea, simplified to 2 dims).
   Need: 2-D Gaussian log-pdf, M-step for (μ_i ∈ ℝ², Σ_i ∈ ℝ²ˣ²),
   either a per-state diagonal Σ or a small Wishart-style update for
   full Σ. Forward-backward and Viterbi unchanged. Student-t version
   needs the multivariate-t scale-mixture EM update — non-trivial
   but well-documented. The single biggest expected-value-of-
   information move because it fixes the "daily returns are the
   wrong feature" diagnosis at the root. The σ channel itself can
   optionally be upgraded from daily-return std to intra-day realised
   variance (see paper 4 "Channel frequency ≠ HMM frequency" note)
   for sharper regime contrast at no change to the HMM frequency or
   sample size; minimal additional cost beyond a one-off hourly-data
   pull. A natural third channel is *drawdown-from-rolling-max*
   d_t = log p_t − max_{s ∈ [t−L, t]} log p_s with L = 200 days —
   stationary, complementary to (r, σ), and the one channel that
   directly carries cumulative-path information (the failure-mode
   diagnosis "regime structure lives at the cumulative-price level"
   from the Phase-0 writeup). See the composite endpoint below for
   the assembled (r, σ, d) trinity.

** Tier 3 — needs new data + new M-step

4. *NH-HMM with VIX in the transition matrix* (papers 2, 3, 4 all
   agree this is the right top of the stack).
   Needs: VIX daily series for the window (free via Yahoo Finance,
   tracked already if we have FRED access); softmax-parameterised
   transition matrix log_A(t) = softmax(β_i · x_t); per-iteration
   M-step is a small logistic regression (Pólya–Gamma or just
   IRLS / scipy.optimize). This is the highest-effort, highest-
   ceiling move. Probably *not* the right Phase-0.5 step — defer to
   Phase-1 if any of the Tier-1 fixes pass.

** Composite Tier 2 + 3 endpoint — recommended assembled setup

If both Tier-1 fixes fail and we escalate, the literature-aligned
endpoint that maximises expected value is *not* a further increase in
K or in ν flexibility; it is a deliberate split of where each
external signal enters the model. The governing principle is:

#+BEGIN_QUOTE
Emission channels are things the regime *produces*. Transition
covariates are things the regime *responds to*.
#+END_QUOTE

Under that principle the four papers cluster cleanly:

| Channel                | Caused by state? | Causes state crossings? | Slot       |
| BTC log-return r_t     | yes              | no                       | emission   |
| BTC realised σ_t       | yes              | no                       | emission   |
| BTC drawdown d_t       | yes (path-dep.)  | no                       | emission   |
| BTC log-volume v_t     | mostly yes       | only at extremes         | emission   |
| VIX                    | no               | yes (macro stress)       | transition |
| DXY / USD index        | no               | yes                      | transition |
| 10Y Treasury yield     | no               | yes                      | transition |
| BTC hash, halving      | no / scheduled   | partially                | transition |

This dissolves the apparent tension between paper 1's covariates-in-
mean approach and paper 3's covariates-in-transition approach: they
are not competing parameterisations of the same idea but different
slots for different *kinds* of variable. Paper 1 puts VIX in the
emission mean, which is mechanically allowed but a weaker generative
claim (VIX is a manifestation of regime); paper 3 puts VIX in
transitions (VIX is a driver of regime crossings) and gets posterior
inclusion probability 1.00 across BTC/ETH/XRP.

*** Recommended assembled setup

- *Emission vector*: y_t = (r_t, σ_t^realised, d_t^drawdown).
  - r_t: daily log-return (already in notebook).
  - σ_t: 5-day realised volatility, optionally upgraded to intra-day
    realised variance per the paper 4 "Channel frequency ≠ HMM
    frequency" note.
  - d_t: drawdown from 200-day rolling max (see "drawdown gotcha"
    below). Captures cumulative path information that (r, σ) cannot.
- *State count*: K = 3 with the (r, σ, d) trinity should suffice —
  paper 4 lands on K = 3 with multivariate observations precisely
  because the extra channels do the discriminating work that paper
  3's K = 4 was buying on scalar returns. Keep K = 4 as a robustness
  variant; do not pre-commit.
- *Emission family*: full-covariance Gaussian first (≈ 9 + 18 = 27
  emission params at K = 3, D = 3 — safe at 759 obs). Student-t
  upgrade as a robustness check; use the multivariate-t scale-
  mixture EM with bounds [3, 30] on ν per state, as in the current
  notebook's univariate version.
- *Transition input*: x_t = (1, VIX_t). Softmax-parameterised
  log A_{ij}(t) = softmax_j(β_{ij} · x_t). Optionally add the 10Y
  Treasury yield (paper 3 posterior inclusion 0.90); DXY if going
  beyond.
- *Frequency*: stay daily for the latent chain (preserves the 2022-
  bear-detection question and the Phase-0 comparison frame). Only
  the σ channel may consume intra-day data.
- *Pass criterion*: unchanged (median bear-posterior over 2022 ≥ 0.6,
  Viterbi flips ≤ 6, gap ≥ 0.15 OR flip-reduction ≥ 30 %). The rule
  worked once; do not retune it after seeing fresh results.

*** Drawdown gotcha (worth flagging explicitly)

Drawdown d_t depends on what is in its rolling-max window, which
makes the channel sensitive to the lookback choice:

- *Expanding max from window start*: d_t never "forgets" the all-
  time high. Carries a structural break at every new ATH and is
  technically non-stationary — HMM EM will tend to bind one state
  to "regime where d_t carries memory of the 2021 peak". Not
  recommended.
- *Rolling max with fixed lookback L*: bounded memory, stationary,
  recommended. L = 200 days is the default. Shorter L (50–100 days)
  emphasises recent drawdowns and is more responsive but loses the
  "deep multi-quarter bear" signal we want for 2022. Longer L (365
  days) preserves it but at the cost of slower edge response.

L = 200 is a deliberate match to the bear-regime duration we're
trying to detect.

*** Effort estimate

Tier 2 emission upgrade (univariate → trivariate Gaussian): ~250
lines as estimated for the bivariate case, plus ~30 lines for the
drawdown computation and lookback handling.

Tier 3 transition upgrade (constant A → VIX-driven softmax A): ~150
lines for the logistic-regression M-step (scipy IRLS is enough;
Pólya-Gamma not needed unless going full Bayesian), plus a one-time
VIX fetch (~20 lines, ~yfinance.download("^VIX")~).

Combined Tier 2+3 is ~450 lines on top of the existing notebook —
comparable to the existing code size and tractable in a single
follow-up session.

** What we should *not* change in the follow-up

- Pass criterion. The 2022-bear-posterior ≥ 0.6 / flips ≤ 6 / gap-or-
  reduction ≥ 0.15 rule worked: it correctly flagged the original run
  as a substantive FAIL despite the rule-based "BORDERLINE". Keep it.
- Restart strategy. 1D-aware k-means + jitter is fine; for multivariate
  observations we get richer init for free.
- Window. 2021-06 → 2023-06 is the test case the original plan
  committed to. Even though paper 3 uses a longer window, expanding
  ours would conflate the methodology question (does the *fix* work)
  with a data-availability question.
- Comparison frame. Apples-to-apples Gaussian vs Student-t under
  shared forward-backward / init / restart / convergence rule. This
  is what made the original notebook's verdict credible despite
  being negative; the follow-up should keep it.
